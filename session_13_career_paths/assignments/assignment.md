## Part 4 — Your First Semester Project

Pick one project idea that you could realistically build during your first semester at NITW. It should meet these criteria:

- **Realistic:** You could start it in the first month with your current skills
- **Completable:** A working version in 12–15 weeks of part-time work
- **Original:** Not a tutorial, not a copy of an existing tool — something with at least one genuinely novel element
- **Demonstrable:** When you finish, you can show it to a recruiter or interviewer and walk them through it

Describe the project in 5 sentences:

**Sentence 1:** What the project does (plain language, no jargon)
**Sentence 2:** What problem it solves or question it answers, and for whom
**Sentence 3:** Which technologies you will use and why (not because they are cool — because they are right for this problem)
**Sentence 4:** What the hardest part will be and how you plan to approach it
**Sentence 5:** How someone else will be able to see or use what you built (a GitHub repo with a working demo, a deployed URL, a short video)

## Part 5 — The Most Important Question

**Length:** Write as much or as little as you actually mean. Do not pad.

Here is the honest question:

*"What part of computing made you genuinely curious during this curriculum, regardless of career prospects?"*

This is not asking what was most useful, what will earn you the most money, or what impressed your parents. It is asking what made you want to understand more — just because it interested you.

It might be: how binary numbers actually represent everything. The moment a trained model made a correct prediction. The fact that a few dozen bytes of binary instructions can produce a result on screen. The way the quant backtester found patterns in data that no one explicitly programmed. The strangeness of a language model that can write code without understanding it in the way humans do. It could be anything.

Why does this question matter? Because over a 30-year career, the work that sustains you — the projects you stay late to finish, the problems you think about in the shower, the things you read on weekends — will almost always be in the area of your genuine curiosity, not your calculated career choice. The people who become truly excellent at what they do almost universally describe being pulled by curiosity, not pushed by career strategy.

This is not an argument to chase your passion and ignore economics. It is an argument to know what your curiosity is, because it will compound into expertise faster than anything you are merely disciplined about.

Answer honestly. This question will not be marked against a rubric. It will be read as writing — for specificity, for honesty, and for signs that you actually thought about it.
